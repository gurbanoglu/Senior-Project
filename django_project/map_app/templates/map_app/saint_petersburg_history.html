Founding of Saint Petersburg May 12, 1703

Initially what today is known as Saint Petersburg, was first settled in 1611 by Swedish pioneers who had built a fortress called Nyenskans located at the mouth of the Neva River. Surrounding Nyenskans, a settlement called "Nyen" began flourishing. Russian monarch or "Tsar" Peter the Great developed an interest in adding a seaport, so that Russia could trade more efficiently with the rest of Europe. Russia's main seaport at the end of the 17th century was Arkhangelsk. Arkhangelsk was geographically far from the rest of Europe and was inaccessible during the ice-covered winters. On May 12, 1703, during the Great Northern War, Peter the Great captured the Nyenskans fortress from the Swedes and named the city "Saint Petersburg" in an attempt to westernise Russia.


Smolny Institute of Noble Maidens 18th Century

Peter the Great declared Saint Petersburg as the Capital of Russia in 1712 which it would remain until 1918. Being one of Russia's most progressive cities, the Institute for Noble Maidens was established in 1764 where girls of noble birth would be granted an education under Catherine the Great. The significance of this can be understood by explaining that this was the first public educational institution for women in not just Russia, but in all of Europe. It formed the bedrock for female education in Russia.








Emancipation of Serfs 1861

In 1861, Tsar Alexander II abolished serfdom throughout the entire Russian Empire. In the coming years, Saint Petersburg would see an influx of former peasants who had previously lived in rural areas. Slums developed on the outskirts of the city. By then the industrial revolution had been well under way, and Saint Petersburg saw its population exceed that of Moscow. It became one of the largest industrial cities in Europe.











Political Revolution Early 20th Century

Mass political and social unrest beginning in Saint Petersburg around January 22nd 1905 led to what is now referred to as the Russian Revolution of 1905 where workers, peasants, and military mutineers directed their anger at Tsar Nicholas II as well as the Russian noble ruling class. In response, Tsar Nicholas II executed the "October Manifesto" which reformed Russia from having an absolute monarchy to a semi-constitutional monarchy by 1906.
The reforms failed to prevent or solve many of the economic issues facing Russian workers: overcrowded unsanitary housing, eleven-hour workdays six days a week, and poverty wages. Only 20% of rural peasants owned their own land while 1.5% of the population owned a staggering 25% of Russia's land. To receive land that they had been working on, peasants were still required to make redemption payments to the monarchy until 1907.
Similar to the 1905 revolution, the first revolt of the Russian Revolution sprung up in Saint Petersburg in February of 1917. Only this time, the revolutionaires known as the "Bolsheviks" would successfully overthrow the rule of Tsar Nicholas II as well as the provisional government that followed later on in October 1917.
As an outcome of the October Revolution of 1917, the Bolshevik government moved the capital of Russia to Moscow which it has remained ever since. Throughout that pivotal time, the country experienced extraordinary instability from civil war to economic catastrophe and famine.









Name of the City

Originally the city was known as "Saint Petersburg" from 1703 until 1914 when it was changed to "Petrograd" as a reaction by the Tsarist government to the war with Germany during World War One.
When the leader of the Russian Revolution Vladimir Lenin passed away in 1924, the city's name was changed again from "Petrograd" (city of Peter) to "Leningrad" (city of Lenin) to honour the deceased leader.
Seventy-seventy seven years later, as the USSR began to fall apart, the city's name returned to its traditional "Saint Petersburg" in 1991 and has kept that name since then.
