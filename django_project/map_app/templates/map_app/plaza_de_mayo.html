Plaza de Mayo

Being the oldest public square in Buenos Aires, Plaza de Mayo is
sacred to the city's native residents. The square has experienced
important events throughout history. From the second founding of
the city in 1580 through the revolution of independence in 1810,
also known as the May Revolution of Buenos Aires, the plaza has
seen both vehement protest or jubilant celebration. Argentina's
first political rally was held here in 1890. Plaza de Mayo
represents the scars and advancements of Argentina's history.

Initially, the plaza was known as Plaza De Victoria, and was built
in 1580 during the founding of Buenos Aires by Juan de Garay.
Upon, independence, the name later changed to "Plaza de Mayo"
to commemorate the 1810 May revolution where Argentina successfully
achieved independence from Spanish colonialism. Every Thursday
afternoon, Plaza de Mayo is the focal point for a group known as
the "Madres de la Plaza de Mayo" (Mothers of the Plaza de Mayo)
assemble to hold pictures of their children who disappeared during
the military dictatorship that ruled Argentina from 1976 to 1983.
Nearby, the Metropolitan Cathedral of Buenos Aires can be visited.
