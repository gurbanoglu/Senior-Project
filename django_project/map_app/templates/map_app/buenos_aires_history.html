First European To Reach The Area of Buenos Aires 1516

Sixteenth century navigator and explorer Juan Díaz de Solís was the first European to reach the Río de la Plata which is the estuary that connects Buenos Aires to the Atlantic ocean. He was representing the Kingdom of Spain at the time. On February 2nd, 1536 a Spanish expediton led by Pedro de Mendoz named the settlement as "Ciudad de Nuestra Señora Santa María del Buen Ayre" meaning "City of Our Lady Saint Mary of the Fair Winds".




An Economy Found On Trade 17th Century

Since its founding, Buenos Aires depended on trade imports from the Kingdom of Spain. Due to the high risk of sea pirates seizing goods from Spanish ships, the Spanish government directed all imports to a city in Peru called Lima which was over 2000 miles away from Buenos Aires. As a result goods took a lengthy amount of time to arrive in Buenos Aires.




Buenos Aires Becomes An Open Port City Late 1700s

A relatively inefficient system of trade imports led to a developing contraband industry between Argentines and the Portuguese. Acknowledging the unintended consequences of his government's trade policy, Charles III of Spain officially made Buenos Aires an open port by the end of the eighteenth century which removed many of the previous restrictions on commerce.





May Revolution May 18-25th, 1810

Following the worsening military conflict in Spain, the May Revolution sprung up in Buenos Aires, being the capital of the Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata during that time. This marked the start of the Argentine War of Independence and acclerated Argentina towards reaching the point of independence on July 9, 1816. Buenos Aires experienced all of the Spanish American Wars of independence without being recaptured once by any armies loyal to the Spanish monarch.






A Restoration of Democratic Elections December 10th, 1983

In 1976, a coup d'état orchestrated by General Jorge Rafael Videla overthrew president Isabel Perón. It is estimated that between 1974 and 1983, up to as many as 30,000 political dissidents were either killed or disappeared at the hands of state terrorism. Economic turmoil, broad dissatisfaction, and a mismanagement of the Falklands territorial conflict, led to the end of the military junta or "military commitee" ruling Argentina. With the 1983 general election, a reemergence of democractic institutions eventually persisted alongside economic growth.
