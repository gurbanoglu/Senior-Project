Palace Square (Dvortsovaya Ploshchad)

Similar to the Red Square in Moscow, the Palace Square has been
a location which has witnessed events throughout history that
have had a profound effect on Russian society. Today, military
parades, sporting events, and concerts are held in the square.
Individuals dressed up as characters from the Tsarist era can be
seen standing next to vintage horse carriages offering tourists a
a ride. Surrounding the square lies the Winter Palace facing the
General Staff Building, a museum on the opposite side. In the
center stands the soaring Alexander Column to commemorate Russia's
victory against the strategic invasion of French Emperor Napoleon
Bonaparte in 1812.

In spite of the magesticity of the site, the square has beared
the brunt of dark days in Russian history. This is where an
unsuccessful assassination attempt against Tsar Alexander II
took place on April 2nd, 1879. Twenty-six years later, royal
guards would open fire on weaponless demonstrators killing over
a hundred civilians and paving the path for the first Russian
Revolution of 1905.

On October 25th, 1917, Bolshevik paramilitary forces gathered in
the square and successfully went on to sack the Winter Palace
bringing an end to hundreds of years of Tsarist rule in Russia.

One of the organizers of the sacking was Moisei Uritsky who would
eventually be assassinated on August 30th, 1918 near the entrace
to the General Staff Building. Thus, from 1918 to 1944 the Palace
Square was was referred to as "Uritsky Square" as a sign of respect
to the organizer.
