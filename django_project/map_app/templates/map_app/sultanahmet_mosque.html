Sultanahmet Mosque

Frequently referred to as the "Blue Mosque", the Sultanahmet
Mosque, received its name from its greatest comissioner: Sultan
Ahmet I. It was ordered to be built in 1609, during the time
that the Ottomans were enduring a crushing loss in their war
with Persia. Historians see this as a decision motivated by
reaffirming Ottoman influence.

Built on what once was a palace for Byzantine Emporers, the mosque
faces the Hagia Sophia, a structure constructed during Byzantine
rule. Gazing upon the Sultanahmet mosque, it may be hard to believe
that its completion took only seven years from 1609-1616. Presently,
the land that the mosque sits on what is known as the Sultanahmet
Square.



The mosque is composed of two different architectural styles:
traditional Islamic architecture and Byzantine architecture.
Venturing into the mosque, one can spot twenty-thousand Iznik
tiles consisting of sixty distinct tulip patterns absorbing
the sunlight through the 260 stained glass windows. Six minarets
tower the city of Ä°stanbul at a noticeable 210 feet. Each minaret
contains two or three balconies where a man called the "muezzin"
makes the Islamic call to prayer five times every day.

The roof of the Sultanahmet mosque is made up of one central dome
positioned 141 feet above ground, bordered by four other domes, as
well as eight lower-level domes.
