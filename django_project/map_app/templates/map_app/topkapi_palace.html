Topkapı Palace

The Topkapı Palace is a museum today, but at one point it served as
the primary residence for Ottoman sultans (monarchs of the former
Ottoman Empire). Six years after the Ottomans conquered the city of
Constantinople, Sultan Mehmet II ordered the construction of the
palace in 1459. He would reside here until his final days in 1481.
From then on, the Topkapı Palace would house twenty-five Sultans until
the mid-19th century. The Sultan would have multiple concubines or
women he would sleep with who were of lower status than his multiple
wives. These concubines would stay in the harem along with the wives
of the Sultan. Men not related to the Sultan were forbidden from
entering the harem unless they were eunuchs (castrated men employed
to guard women's living quarters).

For six-hundred years, thirty Ottoman Sultans would make critical
decisions with input coming from their Grand Vizier (highest ranking
minister) and other council members. Meetings between the council
and the Sultan were initially held in the Imperial Court inside the
Topkapı Palace, but the newly established Dolmabahçe Palace became
the meeting location for these council meetings in mid-19th century
at the request of Sultan Abdülmecid I.


In its heyday, the Topkapı was a city-palace with a population of
about 4,000 residents, covering an area of 70 hectares or 173
acres. As a consequence of conquering the land from the Byzantine
Empire, much was inherited, including the Church of Hagia Eirene.
Located in the first courtyard, the Ottomans transformed this
church into an armory.

The second courtyard referred to as "Divan Meydanı" in Turkish,
acted as the seat of the Divan (Imperial Council). Only those who
needed to contact the Imperial Council for business affairs were
permitted inside. If the walls around this courtyard could speak,
they could narrate a lengthy passage of all the administrative and
political resolutions which fundamentally altered the course of
world history.

Possessing the highest concentraton of Ottoman art, jewels, and
antiques, the Imperial Treasury is a quarter that mustn't be
left out when visiting. An iron coat worn by Sultan Mustafa III,
the Topkapı Dagger, and two enormous solid gold candleholders,
each weighing 105 lbs with 6,666 mounted cut diamonds, can be
seen scattered across multiple exhibits.
